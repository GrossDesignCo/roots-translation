# Difficult Words with Fuzzy/In-Progress Translations

Generally this translation side-steps difficult word choices by simply rendering _a_ word and then consistently using it to build a new semantic meaning from context, however there are some words or word-sets that prove difficult regardless.

Usually this is due to incomplete action/verb/adjective/object/subject sets in English where either one or more words have been dropped over the years or the concept never developed as fully.

For example, "An inventor invents an invention", however the adjective "holy" has no direct verb for "to make holy" or "to be holy", nor a noun for a "holy thing".

## El / Elohe / Elohim

God is handled in several ways in Hebrew, this translation attempts to distinguish clearly between them. In the literal-english it preserves the plural "Gods" everywhere, regardless of which Gods the text is talking about. It's often paired with masculine singular verbs, which is helpful in understanding, but may be especially confusing for newer English readers, hence the natural-english uses "God" singular always.

In some cases "El" is used, especially in the construction of names. Currently this translation keeps "God" singular, but is almost closer to "G—", though this doesn't quite feel satisfactory.

For example, Eleazer is translated "God-my-Help", even though "G—my-Help" would likely be more appropriate.

## YHWH / YahWeh / Yah

Similarly, the name of the Lord is frequently shortened/modified when combining it with other words.

Currently this translation uses "He—" (emdash) for "Yah", especially when used in names to indicate which "he" is being referenced.

YahWeh is a unique construction of the verb hayah / to be. Notably it's similar to the third person "he is" but distinct from it. This translation uses "He-Who-Is" in a similar naming convention to other names.

## Holy / Sanctify (קדשׁ)

The root קדשׁ appears in multiple parts of speech that need distinct dictionary entries:

| Form | Root key | Type | Gloss | Usage |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| קָדוֹשׁ | `qadosh` | adjective | holy | "holy, sacred, set apart" — describes a quality |
| קדשׁ | `qadash` | verb | to sanctify | "to make holy, set apart" — the action of consecrating |
| קֹדֶשׁ | `qodesh` | noun | holiness | "holiness, sacredness" — the state/quality itself |

When translating: adjective forms (קָדוֹשׁ) use "holy", verb forms (קִדֵּשׁ, etc.) use "sanctify", noun forms (קֹדֶשׁ) use "holiness".

## Amen

Jesus's famous "Truly truly..." statements start with the Greek "Amen amen...".

It might be best to use "amen" since it's a common enough English word, but that is also a generally religious word with no common meaning in most people's minds.

Other challenging liturgical words to address:

- Congregation/Ecclesia
- Blessed?
- Holy/Sacred/Set-Apart
- Sabbath

## Adam / Adamah / Adom

Currently "Human" and "Soil" are separate, but a connection should be indicated here somehow.

On a literary level, "Adam" is more like "Earthling", but that sounds too space-age.

"Earthen-One" and "Earth" might cover this better.

Adom likewise means "Red", like the iron-rich soil/clay.

## Arum / Arom

Currently Shrewd and Naked are separate, but the words share the same root consonants, and have a thematic link, which should be indicated somehow.

Possibly more like Naked and "Naked-sight", where the Shrewd sees right through what is covered. This may be a thematic link, but the wordplay is lost in English.
